Operational Technology

Why Operational Technology Security Requires a Different Approach

Operational technology systems present security challenges that go beyond protecting information and maintaining conventional business services.

By Elliot Whittaker

23 July 2026

Approximately 8 minutes

Working with and securing operational technology (OT) often requires a different approach from assessing a typical enterprise system.

OT presents different challenges depending on its use case. It could be an aircraft electronic flight bag, an oil and gas SCADA system or a railway traffic-monitoring system. However, these systems share the same overarching requirement: they must operate safely and predictably, even when a fault or compromise occurs.

Enterprise security objectives

Enterprise systems often share a familiar set of high-level security objectives. Security is commonly motivated by an organisation's need to reduce financial losses from downtime, regulatory and legal penalties, loss of sensitive information and damage to an organisation’s competitiveness or reputation.

While these objectives can also apply to operational technology, failures in operational environments can create much more serious consequences.

When failure affects the physical world

When a system is responsible for managing hospital life-support equipment, an aircraft engine or a critical industrial process, the consequences extend far beyond financial loss.

Security decisions must therefore consider the potential effect on people, physical equipment, the environment and the continued delivery of essential services.

Reducing both likelihood and impact

When designing or assuring an OT system, the focus expands beyond identifying how the likelihood of compromise can be reduced. It must also consider how the impact can be limited when a compromise or fault does occur.

The impact of compromise or failure should therefore be considered early, with appropriate continuity, recovery, redundancy and fail-safe measures incorporated into the system design.

Security must support safe operation

Security controls cannot be considered in isolation from operational requirements. A control that improves confidentiality but prevents an operator from responding to an emergency may introduce more risk than it removes.

Effective OT security therefore requires a balanced and risk-based approach. Security, safety, availability, resilience and operational performance must all be considered together.

A lesson from design assurance

During a design review, I assessed a component that played an important role within a wider system architecture. The design incorporated defence-in-depth which, on paper, appeared to provide robust protection against a broad range of threats.

However, one area had received comparatively little attention.

When I asked the design team how the system would respond to an internal error or component failure, the discussion exposed a significant risk. Considerable effort had been invested in protecting the system against external threats, but much less consideration had been given to the possibility of the system itself failing.

The review highlighted an important lesson: security architecture and assurance can become heavily focused on resisting external threats, while safe failure, recovery and degraded operation receive far less attention.

Assurance cannot rely on the assumption that a well-designed system will always operate as intended. It must also consider how the system behaves when components fail, controls malfunction or expected operating conditions are no longer available.

Conclusion

Operational technology security is not simply enterprise cyber security applied to industrial equipment. It requires an understanding of how digital compromise can create physical and operational consequences.

By considering the consequences of failure early and designing systems to operate safely and predictably under adverse conditions, organisations can build security that supports rather than obstructs the operational mission.
